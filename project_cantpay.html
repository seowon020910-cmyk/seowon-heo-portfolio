Background & Objective

Adapting a political comedy for Korean audiences

A political farce exploring working-class resistance through satire and physical comedy. As Assistant Director, I supported scene development, rehearsals, blocking, and production coordination for the adaptation of the English script into a fully staged Korean performance.

I chose this work as my graduation project because its satire and social commentary resonate deeply with contemporary issues.

Notably, as there was no dedicated props team, I also designed and fabricated all props myself — including custom food labels designed in Illustrator and Photoshop.

"The role of an assistant director extends far beyond supporting tasks — it requires shaping the overall direction, atmosphere, and communicative clarity of the performance."

Process

Direction, translation, and production

  • 01

    Script Translation (ENG → KOR)

    Translated the full English script into Korean — adapting comedic rhythm, political nuance, and character tone without losing the original farcical energy. Sourced the original English script, compared it with the rehearsal draft, re-translated problematic lines, and shared final pages with the directing and production team. Used extensively by actors and the director during all rehearsals.

    Full Korean translation of Can't Pay? Won't Pay!

  • 02

    Scene Breakdown & Blocking

    Broke down the script into scene units with detailed information for cast and staff. Created early blocking sketches for farce-style movement and door-comedy rhythm. Coordinated blocking with props, timing cues, and set entrances/exits. Ensured actor safety and spatial clarity for high-energy scenes.

    Scene breakdown and blocking diagrams

  • 03

    Rehearsal Notes & Weekly Logs

    Documented rehearsal hours, actor comments, and schedule adjustments in a shared log — ensuring actors could review director notes, staff could track scene progress, and schedule updates were communicated clearly. This log became the central reference for all creative and technical decisions.

    Weekly rehearsal notes and logs

  • 04

    Venue Scouting & Safety Assessment

    Conducted a full venue walkthrough of Namsan Drama Centre to check space-specific restrictions, safety requirements, and audience flow feasibility. This allowed the team to anticipate constraints and adapt blocking and tech operations accordingly.

    Site inspection notes for Namsan Drama Centre

  • 05

    Props Design & Fabrication

    With no dedicated props team, I managed all props preparation and distribution. Fabricated a custom sofa cover from costume workshop fabric to disguise supermarket bags containing stage items. Designed original era-appropriate food labels (Supermeat cans, Canary Feed, Rabbit Meat packaging) using Illustrator and Photoshop, printed and applied to stage-safe containers.

    Custom props fabrication and original food label designs

Achievements & Reflections

What I learned

This graduation production allowed me to reconnect with the fundamentals of direction — carrying the weight of unifying multiple strands of interpretation into one cohesive vision. As assistant director, I participated in prop design, costume adjustments, rehearsal planning, and shaping the overall visual and emotional rhythm of the production. I realised that the role of an assistant director requires shaping the overall direction, atmosphere, and communicative clarity of the performance — not just completing assigned tasks. This experience reinforced my commitment to developing as a director who can navigate artistic vision, collaborative processes, and meaningful storytelling with intention and confidence.
